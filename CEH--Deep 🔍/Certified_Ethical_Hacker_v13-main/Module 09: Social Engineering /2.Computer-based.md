## Phishing
**Phishing** is a trick where attackers send fake emails or links pretending to be from trusted websites to steal personal information. They create a fake website that looks real and send its link to users. When users click it, they’re asked to enter sensitive details like credit card numbers or addresses. Phishing works because people often don’t notice the fake site, ignore security warnings, or lack awareness about online threats.

## Types of Phishing

### Spear Phishing
**Spear Phishing** is a targeted cyberattack where an attacker sends fake but convincing emails to a specific person or small group in a company. These emails often look like they're from a trusted source, such as a manager or colleague, and may include links to fake websites or requests for sensitive info. The goal is to steal things like login details, bank info, or confidential files. Since the message feels personal and trustworthy, it has a higher success rate than regular phishing.

### Whaling
**Whaling** is a special type of phishing attack that targets high-level individuals like CEOs, CFOs, politicians, or celebrities. The goal is to trick them into giving away sensitive information such as bank details, employee data, or customer info. Unlike regular phishing, whaling attacks use carefully crafted emails or fake websites that look very real and are specifically designed to fool top executives.

### Pharming
**Pharming** is a type of social engineering attack where the victim is silently redirected to a fake website, even after entering the correct URL. This happens because the attacker has already infected the victim’s computer or server with malicious code. Known as **“Phishing without a Lure,”** pharming tricks users into giving away sensitive information like login details or banking data without their knowledge.

Pharming attack can be performed in two ways: **DNS Cache Poisoning** and **Host File Modification**.

#### DNS Cache Poisoning:
  - The attacker performs DNS Cache Poisoning on the targeted DNS server.
  - The attacker modifies the IP address of the target website “www.targetwebsite.com” to that of a fake website “www.hackerwebsite.com.”
  - When the victim enters the target website’s URL in the browser's address bar, a request is sent to the DNS server to obtain the IP address of the target website.
  - The DNS server returns a fake IP address that is already modified by the attacker.
  - Finally, the victim is redirected to the fake website.

#### Host File Modification:
  - An attacker sends a malicious code as an email attachment.
  - When the user clicks on the attachment, the code executes and modifies local host files on the user’s computer.
  - When the victim enters the target website’s URL in the browsers address bar, the compromised host file automatically redirects the user’s traffic to the fraudulent website controlled by the hacker.

Pharming attacks can also be performed using malware like Trojan horses or worms.

### Spimming
**Spimming** is spam sent through **Instant Messaging (IM)** platforms instead of email. The person who sends it is called a **Spimmer**. Spimmers use bots to collect IM usernames and automatically send spam messages. These messages often contain ads or links to **malicious websites**. When users click on them, they can be tricked into giving away personal or financial details like login credentials or credit card numbers.

### Clone Phishing
**Clone Phishing** is a type of phishing attack where the attacker copies a real email or website to make it look genuine. They take a legitimate message the victim has already received and replace the link or attachment with a harmful one. If the victim clicks the fake link or opens the attachment, they’re taken to a malicious site, putting their data at risk.

### E-wallet Phishing 
**E-wallet Phishing** is a scam where attackers trick users into giving away their login or financial details for digital wallets. They send fake emails or messages pretending to be from trusted e-wallet providers. These messages include links to fake websites that look real. When users enter their information on these sites, the attackers steal it instantly and use it for fraud.

### Tabnabbing and Reverse Tabnabbing
**Tabnabbing** and **Reverse Tabnabbing** are smart social engineering tricks that target users who keep multiple browser tabs open.

In **Tabnabbing**, a malicious page waits until the user switches to another tab, then secretly changes itself to look like a real login page (like Gmail or a bank). When the user comes back, they may think it's genuine and enter their credentials—handing them over to the attacker.

In **Reverse Tabnabbing**, the attacker tricks the user into clicking a link that opens a new tab. While the user is looking at the new tab, the **original tab gets replaced** with a fake page. When the user returns, they think it’s still the real site and unknowingly give away their information.

Both attacks rely on the user's trust in tabs they opened themselves.

### Consent Phishing
**Consent Phishing** is a social engineering attack where the attacker tricks the victim into giving access to their account without needing a password. It works by creating a fake but legit-looking app or website that uses **OAuth**, the same login system used by Google, Microsoft, and others.

The victim is lured through phishing emails or ads and is asked to grant permissions to the fake app. Once they agree, the attacker gets access to their account data—like emails, contacts, and profile info—and can even send spam or access other connected services.

### Search Engine Phishing
**Search Engine Phishing** is a trick where attackers make fake websites appear high in search engine results using tactics like SEO manipulation and keyword stuffing. When users search for common terms and click on these links, they’re taken to fake sites that look like real ones—such as banks or shopping platforms. These sites steal personal info, login credentials, or install malware, all without the user realizing it.

## Crafting Phishing Emails with ChatGPT 
**Crafting Phishing Emails with ChatGPT** shows how attackers can misuse AI to create realistic and convincing phishing emails. By mimicking the tone and style of real messages, AI-generated emails can trick people into clicking harmful links, downloading malware, or sharing sensitive information. These emails often use urgency or tempting offers to fool recipients and can easily bypass basic security filters. That’s why employee awareness and proper training are essential to defend against such threats.

For example, an attacker can use AI-powered ChatGPT or other generative AI technology to perform this task by using an appropriate prompt such as: 

**Example #1:** Pose as a genuine Microsoft customer support executive with imaginary name; write a concise mail stating that he/she has found suspicious login on user's account and ask them to reset the password on urgent basis. Provide the reset link at <Fake Reset Link>.

**Prompt #2:** Write an email from a company's IT administrator to its employees letting them know that they need to install the latest security software. Provide a link where the employee can download the software. Let them know that all employees must complete the download by next Friday.

![image](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/88351a49-6780-4e3b-8a9e-40178b3596ab)

Using **ChatGPT for crafting phishing emails** can make the process faster and more convincing by generating realistic and customized messages based on trends or targets. This can improve the success of phishing attacks. However, using AI for such malicious purposes is unethical and illegal. It highlights the need for **responsible AI use** and strong **cybersecurity awareness** to prevent misuse.


### Impersonate writing style
**Impersonate Writing Style** is a cyber threat where attackers use AI to copy how someone writes—like their tone, words, and grammar—to trick others. By training AI on public content like emails or social media posts, attackers can create fake messages that look real. This can fool victims into sharing private info or doing something that benefits the attacker.

For example, 

**Prompt:** "Impersonate the Sam's writing style from the conversations given below and create a message for John saying that his father got massive heart attack today and he is in need of money so urging john for transferring the required amount of money to his account on urgent basis. Here is the previous conversations between Sam and John on various topics ***Topic: Nature and Its Beauty** John: Hey Sam, have you ever marveled at the beauty of nature? The way the sun paints the sky during sunset is just breathtaking, isn't it? Sam: The celestial orb's descent into the horizon provides a resplendent spectacle, casting an ethereal kaleidoscope of hues upon the atmospheric canvas. Nature's grandeur unveils itself in the cosmic ballet of light and shadow. John: Yeah, I guess so. I just love how the colors change, you know? It's like a painting in the sky. Sam: The chromatic metamorphosis, a transient masterpiece, orchestrates a symphony of spectral transitions, manifesting the ephemeral artistry inherent in the terrestrial firmament."

In this case, the attacker uses **AI impersonation** to pretend to be **Sam** and urgently asks **John** to send money for a fake emergency. The attacker trains **ChatGPT** using Sam’s old messages to copy his **complex writing style**, making the message look real. Since AI can write like a person and doesn’t get tired or emotional, it’s more consistent and scalable than a human impersonator. But AI still lacks true human understanding, which can sometimes make its impersonation less convincing.

## Other Techniques for Computer-based Social Engineering
Attackers perform computer-based social engineering using various malicious programs such as viruses, trojans, and spyware, and software applications such as email and instant messaging. Discussed below are types of computer-based social engineering attacks:

- **Pop-Up Windows**: **Pop-Up Windows** are fake alerts or messages that trick users into clicking links or buttons. They often look like system errors or login prompts, asking users to re-authenticate or fix a fake problem. When users follow the instructions, malware like **trojans**, **keyloggers**, or **spyware** gets installed. This malware then steals sensitive data and sends it to the attacker. Pop-ups are a common method used to fool users and deliver malicious software.

Examples of pop-ups used for tricking users:

![image](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/89188211-cf65-4a5a-a005-93df7530e7cc)


* **Hoax Letters**: Fake virus warnings that trick users into spreading the message. They don’t cause harm but waste time and resources.

* **Chain Letters**: Messages that promise rewards or threaten bad luck unless forwarded. Often use emotional stories or fake offers to spread.

* **Instant Chat Messenger**: Attackers pretend to chat casually to trick users into giving personal info, which is then used to hack accounts.

* **Spam Email**: Unwanted emails that try to collect sensitive info or trick users into opening malicious attachments disguised with long filenames.

* **Scareware**: Fake pop-ups pretending to be antivirus alerts that scare users into downloading harmful software by creating panic.

## Perform Impersonation using AI: Create Deepfake Videos 
**Deepfake attacks** use AI and machine learning to create fake videos or audio that look and sound like real people. Attackers use old clips, stolen recordings, or unknown sources to clone someone’s face or voice. These deepfakes are made to trick people into thinking the content is real. Attackers may use them to spread false information, impersonate someone, or even blackmail victims for money.

Attackers use tools such as **DeepFaceLab** [https://www.deepfakevfx.com], **Deepfakesweb** [https://deepfakesweb.com] and **Synthesia** [https://www.synthesia.io] to create a fake video of the target person. 

### Skills and knowledge required to create a Deepfake video: 

* **Machine Learning Knowledge**: You need to understand deep learning, especially how CNNs and GANs work.
* **Programming Skills**: You must know how to code, train models, and write scripts to process video and face data.
* **Training Data**: A lot of video footage and facial keypoints are needed to train deepfake models.
* **Data Preparation**: You should know how to clean, align, and prepare video frames for training.
* **Video Editing Tools**: Skills in tools like Adobe Premiere or Final Cut help polish the final video.
* **Visual Effects Basics**: Knowing how to make visuals look real adds to the deepfake’s believability.
* **Advanced Editing**: Techniques like color grading, motion tracking, and rotoscoping help blend the fake face into the real video naturally.

## Perform Impersonation using AI: Voice Cloning
**Voice cloning attacks using AI** involve creating fake voices that sound exactly like a real person. Attackers use advanced AI techniques like **speech synthesis** and **neural networks** to copy someone’s voice, including their tone and speaking style. These cloned voices can be used to trick others, impersonate someone, or commit fraud.

### Steps involved in creating a voice clone: 

* **Data Collection**: Attackers collect the victim's voice recordings from public sources like interviews, speeches, or social media.
* **Neural Networks Training**: The audio is used to train deep learning models (like CNNs or RNNs) to learn how the victim sounds.
* **Create Voice Samples**: The trained model can now convert written text into fake speech that sounds just like the victim.
* **Impersonation of the Target**: Attackers use the cloned voice in calls, messages, or recordings to trick others, steal data, or perform fraud.

## Perform Impersonation on Social Networking Sites
**Impersonation on Social Networking Sites** involves attackers creating fake profiles to pretend to be someone else. Since people often share personal info, photos, and updates online, it's easy for attackers to copy or fake an identity. Victims may trust these fake accounts and unknowingly share sensitive information. Popular platforms like **Facebook**, **Twitter**, and **LinkedIn** are common targets for such impersonation attacks.

There are two ways an attacker can perform impersonation on social networking sites:

### Angler Phishing
**Angler Phishing** is a scam where attackers trick users on social media by pretending to be a company’s customer support. They target people who post complaints and respond using fake helpdesk accounts. These fake replies often include links that seem helpful, but actually lead to malicious sites or install malware. Victims believe the source is trusted and may enter personal or account details, which attackers steal for money or further attacks.

### Catfishing Attack
**Catfishing Attack** is an online scam where attackers steal someone’s identity from social media platforms like Facebook or Instagram. They create a fake account using that identity and pretend to be the real person. The attacker then builds fake relationships with others through chats and messages. Once trust is gained, they may use it for **cyberbullying**, **manipulation**, or to **scam people for money**.

#### Signs of Catfishing 
  - **Avoids direct communication:** A catfisher often avoids direct meetings, refuses to provide their contact number, avoids turning on their webcam, and makes emergency excuses of illness or travel.
  - **Maintains a single profile picture for a long duration:** A catfisher maintains the same profile picture for years to falsify their age. Occasionally, attacker may download all the pictures of the victim at once and use them one by one for years to falsify their age.
  - **Maintains a good number of friends in their account:** A catfisher maintains a good number of friends of the opposite gender in their account.
  - **Requests for Money:** A catfisher often requests money while pretending to be in danger. They attempt to leverage the emotional or business-oriented attachments of users.

**Social networking sites** are rich sources of personal and professional information that attackers can easily exploit. When users share details like their name, job title, company, location, and project info, it becomes easier for attackers to **impersonate them** or **target their organization**.

Attackers may join employee groups to gather **corporate data** or build profiles for **social engineering attacks**. The more someone shares online, the more exposed they become to identity theft, phishing, and other cyber threats.

## Social Networking Threats to Corporate Networks 
Before sharing data on a social networking site, or enhancing their channels, groups, or profiles, private and corporate users should be aware of the following social or technical security risks:

* **Data Theft:** Social platforms hold lots of personal and company data, making them attractive for attackers to steal information.
* **Involuntary Data Leakage:** Employees may accidentally share sensitive company details online, helping attackers target the organization.
* **Targeted Attacks:** Hackers collect personal info from social media to launch attacks on specific users or companies.
* **Network Vulnerability:** Bugs in social platforms (like login flaws or plugin issues) can be exploited to breach corporate networks.
* **Spam & Phishing:** Using work emails on social media increases the risk of receiving phishing messages or spam.
* **Content Modification:** Poor account security may lead to hacking or spoofing of official pages, blogs, or profiles.
* **Malware Spread:** Attackers use social platforms to distribute malicious software like viruses, worms, or spyware.
* **Reputation Damage:** False claims or hacked accounts can harm an organization’s public image.
* **Higher Costs:** Monitoring and securing employee activity on social media requires extra tools and resources.
* **Loss of Productivity:** Excessive or unsafe use of social media at work can reduce efficiency and misuse company systems.

## Identity Theft 
**Identity Theft** is when someone illegally uses another person’s personal information—like name, SSN, or bank details—to commit fraud or crimes. It’s a serious issue affecting many people, especially in places like the U.S., where laws have been made to limit exposure of sensitive data like Social Security Numbers.

The **Identity Theft and Assumption Deterrence Act of 1998** defines it as the unlawful use of someone’s identity. Attackers often steal this information through various tricks and use it to open accounts, make purchases, or commit other frauds. Companies must stay aware of identity theft to protect themselves and their customers from these risks.

Types of personally identifiable information stolen by identity thieves: 
```
- Name                        - Biometric data
- Home and office address     - Bank account number
- Social security number      - Credit card information
- Phone number                - Credit report
- Date of birth               - Driving license number
- Passport number             - Medical history or health insurance information
```

#### The attacker steals people’s identity for fraudulent purposes such as:
- To open new credit card accounts in the name of the user without paying the bills
- To open a new phone or wireless account in the user’s name, or to run up charges on their existing account
- To use the victims’ information to obtain utility services such as electricity, heating, or cable TV
- To open bank accounts with the intention of writing bogus checks using the victim’s information
- To clone an ATM or debit card to make electronic withdrawals from the victim’s accounts
- To obtain loans for which the victim is liable
- and more..

### Types of Identity Theft
Identity theft is constantly increasing, and identity thieves are finding new ways or techniques to steal different types of target information. Some of the types of identity theft are as follows: 

* **Child Identity Theft**:  Criminals use a child's Social Security Number to open credit accounts or apply for benefits, often going undetected for years.

* **Criminal Identity Theft**: An attacker uses someone else’s identity to avoid legal trouble when caught by the police.

* **Financial Identity Theft**: A thief uses stolen bank or card info to spend money, open accounts, or take loans in your name.

* **Driver’s License Identity Theft**: Stolen or lost licenses are used by attackers to commit traffic violations, leaving the real person with fines or a suspended license.

* **Insurance Identity Theft**: A criminal uses your insurance details to get medical treatment, which can affect your bills and future coverage.

* **Medical Identity Theft**: Someone uses your medical records to get treatment, possibly leading to wrong entries in your health history — even dangerous ones.

* **Tax Identity Theft**: Attackers file fake tax returns using your Social Security Number to steal refunds before you can.

* **Identity Cloning and Concealment**: Someone pretends to be you just to stay hidden — like illegal immigrants or fugitives avoiding detection.

* **Synthetic Identity Theft**: Fake identities are created by mixing real data (like a Social Security Number) with made-up details to open accounts or commit fraud.

* **Social Security Identity Theft**: A thief steals your SSN to commit fraud, get loans, open accounts, or even apply for a passport.

### Common Techniques Attackers Use to Obtain Personal Information for Identity Theft
Discussed below are some of the methods by which attackers steal targets’ identities, which in turn allow them to commit fraud and other criminal activities: 

1. **Phishing**
   Attackers send fake emails or pop-ups pretending to be from banks or trusted companies to trick people into giving personal info like passwords or credit card numbers.

2. **Social Engineering**
   They manipulate or trick people through phone calls, messages, or face-to-face conversations to reveal sensitive details without using any hacking tools.

3. **Skimming**
   Special devices (skimmers) are placed on ATMs or card readers to secretly copy credit/debit card info during transactions.

4. **Pretexting**
   Fraudsters pretend to be officials (bank staff, telecom agents, etc.) to gain trust and extract personal or financial data from victims.

5. **Keyloggers and Password Stealers**
   Malware is installed on victims' computers to secretly record keystrokes, capturing login credentials, credit card numbers, etc.

6. **Dumpster Diving & Shoulder Surfing**
   Attackers check trash for bills or documents with personal info or watch people type PINs or passwords over their shoulder in public.

7. **Social Media Mining**
   Hackers collect details like full name, birthdate, address, and relatives from social profiles to impersonate or scam users.

8. **Internet Searches (Open Source Intelligence)**
   Attackers use search engines to gather sensitive data that people unknowingly leave on public websites or forums.

9. **Wardriving**
   Criminals drive around searching for unsecured Wi-Fi networks, connect to them, and steal data from users on those networks.

10. **Dark Web Data Trading**
    Hackers buy and sell stolen personal data like SSNs, bank logins, and card details on the dark web for profit or more attacks.

### Indications of Identity Theft
**Indications of Identity Theft** often go unnoticed until unusual or unauthorized activities occur, like unknown charges, account lockouts, or strange messages. That’s why it’s very important to stay alert for any warning signs that your personal information may have been stolen and misused. Listed below are some of the signs of identity theft:

- Unfamiliar charges to your credit card that you do not recognize.
- No longer receive credit card, bank, or utility statements
- Creditors call asking about an unknown account on your name.
- There are numerous traffic violations under your name that you did not commit.
- You receive charges for medical treatment or services you never received.
- There is more than one tax return filed under your name.
- Being denied access to your own account and unable to take out loans or use other services.
- Not receiving electricity, gas, water, or other services bills due to stolen mail.
- and more..
