# 👥 Human-based Social Engineering Techniques
**Human-based Social Engineering** is a technique where attackers trick people into giving away sensitive information by pretending to be someone trustworthy, like an IT support technician. They interact directly with employees to gather details like business plans or network access, which can later be used to launch an attack.

An attacker can perform human-based social engineering by using the following techniques: 
```
- Impersonation                          - Tailgating
- Vishing                                - Diversion
- Eavesdropping                          - Theft
- Shoulder Surfing                       - Honey
- Dumpster Diving                        - Trap
- Reverse Social Engineering             - Baiting
- Piggybacking                           - Bait and Switching
- Quid Pro Quo                           - Elicitation
```
---
## Impersonation 
**Impersonation** is a social engineering trick where an attacker pretends to be someone trusted—like a courier, technician, or visitor—to fool people into giving away sensitive information. They may do this in person, over the phone, or through other communication methods. Once inside, they might look for passwords on desks, search bins for documents, eavesdrop on private talks, or watch someone type (shoulder surfing) to steal information without being noticed.

Some impersonation tricks that an attacker performs to gather sensitive information about the target organization exploit the human nature of trust, fear, and moral obligation. 

Types of impersonation used in social engineering:

**1. Posing as a Legitimate End User** - 
The attacker pretends to be a regular employee (like someone from the finance team) and asks for help, such as recovering a "forgotten" password. They use friendliness or sympathy to trick others into giving access.

**2. Posing as an Important User** - 
Here, the attacker pretends to be a high-ranking person like a manager or director. They use authority and urgency to pressure others into giving out sensitive information quickly—sometimes even threatening them if they refuse.

**3. Posing as a Technical Support Agent** - 
The attacker pretends to be from IT support and uses technical jargon to confuse the victim. They may say there’s a system issue and ask for login credentials to "fix" it.

**4. Posing as an Internal Employee, Client, or Vendor** - 
The attacker physically enters the company posing as a contractor or visitor. They look for passwords on desks, listen to conversations, or check trash bins to gather useful information.

**5. Posing as a Repairman** - 
Attackers dress as technicians (like electricians or phone repair staff) and gain access to restricted areas. While pretending to fix something, they secretly search for sensitive data or even plant spying devices.

## Impersonation (Vishing)
**Vishing (Voice Phishing)** is a scam where attackers use **phone calls or VoIP** to trick people into giving away sensitive information like **bank details, credit card numbers, or login credentials**. They often use **caller ID spoofing** to appear as a trusted source, like a bank. Victims may receive a fake **SMS, email, or direct call** asking them to call a number or follow instructions. These calls usually play **pre-recorded messages** that seem official and ask for personal information. Once the victim shares their details, the system confirms it as "verified," but the data goes straight to the attacker for misuse.

Discussed below are some tricks attackers use when Vishing to gather sensitive information.

-  **Abusing the Over-Helpfulness of Help Desk** - 
Attackers call help desks pretending to be employees under pressure (like missing a deadline) and use emotional stories to convince the staff to reset passwords or share sensitive info. Help desk staff, trained to be helpful, often comply without verifying the caller’s identity.

- **Third-party Authorization** - 
Attackers pretend to be someone sent by a senior employee who’s unavailable (like on vacation). By name-dropping and sounding confident, they trick others into sharing information without proper verification, relying on people’s tendency to trust authority references.

- **Tech Support** - 
The attacker pretends to be from IT or a software vendor. They claim there’s a technical issue and ask for login credentials to “fix” it. Believing it’s genuine, employees often share usernames and passwords, giving attackers access to the system.

## Eavesdropping
**Eavesdropping** is when someone secretly listens to or reads private conversations without permission. This can happen through phone calls, emails, chats, or any form of communication. Attackers use this method to steal sensitive information like passwords, business details, phone numbers, and addresses.

---
## Shoulder Surfing 
**Shoulder Surfing** is a method used by attackers to steal sensitive information by watching someone enter it—like a password or PIN—on a device. This can be done by simply looking over the victim’s shoulder or using tools like **binoculars** or **hidden cameras** to record their actions. It's a simple but effective way to gather login details and personal data without the victim knowing.

---
## Dumpster Diving
**Dumpster Diving** is a technique where attackers search through trash to find sensitive information like passwords, bank statements, source code, or personal details. This data can be found on discarded papers, disks, or devices. Attackers may also pretend to be repair workers or cleaners to access trash bins without raising suspicion. They use the collected information to plan and carry out further attacks.

Information that attackers can obtain by searching through trash bins includes: 

* **Phone lists**: Help attackers learn employee names and phone numbers for impersonation or social engineering.
* **Organizational charts**: Show how the company is structured, including sensitive areas like server rooms or restricted zones.
* **Email printouts, notes, faxes, memos**: May contain passwords, personal info, work processes, or internal instructions.
* **Policy manuals**: Give insight into system usage rules, employment policies, and operations—useful for planning attacks.
* **Event notes, calendars, logs**: Help attackers know when users are active or away, so they can choose the best time to attack.

## Reverse Social Engineering
**Reverse Social Engineering** is a tricky attack where the hacker pretends to be a helpful expert. First, they create a problem in the system, then act like they can fix it. This makes employees reach out to them for help. During the conversation, the attacker cleverly asks questions to gather sensitive information. It takes planning, skill, and experience to pull off, but when done well, it can trick people into revealing important details without realizing it.

Provided below are some of the techniques involved in reverse social engineering:
* **Sabotage**: The attacker makes the system look broken or actually damages it, so the victim thinks something is wrong and needs help.
* **Marketing**: The attacker pretends to be a helper by placing their contact info where the victim can see it, like on a fake error message or a business card.
* **Support**: Even after stealing the needed data, the attacker keeps pretending to help the victim so they don’t realize they’ve been tricked.

## Piggybacking
Piggybacking usually implies entry into a building or security area with the consent of the authorized person. For example, an attacker might request an authorized person to unlock a security door, saying that they have forgotten their ID badge. In the interest of common courtesy, the authorized person will allow the attacker to pass through the door.

---
## Tailgating
**Tailgating** is a physical security breach where an attacker sneaks into a secure area by following closely behind an authorized person. They take advantage of politeness, like someone holding the door open, and may wear a fake ID to appear legitimate. The goal is to enter restricted areas without permission by pretending to be authorized.

---
## Diversion Theft 
**Diversion Theft** is a trick used by attackers to mislead delivery workers or companies into sending goods or information to the wrong place. Also called the **“Round the Corner Game,”** this method uses **social engineering** to convince someone—like a van driver—to deliver a package to a fake location.

Online, the same trick can be used to fool people into sending sensitive files to the wrong person. The goal is to **divert the delivery** and **steal the item or data** without raising suspicion.

---
## Honey Trap
The honey trap is a technique where an attacker targets a person online by pretending to be an attractive person and then begins a fake online relationship to obtain confidential information about the target company. In this technique, the victim is an insider who possesses critical information about the target organization. 

---
## Baiting 
**Baiting** is a social engineering trick where attackers lure victims with something tempting—like a USB drive labeled “Employee Salary Information 2024.” They leave it in public places such as elevators or parking lots, hoping someone will plug it into their computer. Once opened, the device installs malware that gives the attacker access to the system. This method takes advantage of human curiosity and greed to steal sensitive data or gain control of a device.

---
## Quid Pro Quo 
**Quid Pro Quo** is a social engineering attack where the hacker offers fake help in exchange for sensitive information. The attacker pretends to be tech support and randomly calls employees in a company. When someone with a real issue responds, the attacker offers to fix it, then tricks the victim into running commands or installing malware. This malware steals confidential data like login credentials. It's a baiting technique that relies on trust and urgency.

---
## Elicitation 
**Elicitation** is a social engineering technique where attackers extract sensitive information through casual and friendly conversations. The goal is to make the victim feel comfortable while unknowingly revealing important details, like usernames or passwords. Attackers use good social skills to steer the conversation toward useful info without raising suspicion. If the attacker doesn’t get the needed data, they continue the process until they do.

---
## Bait and Switching
**Bait and Switching** is a social engineering trick where attackers lure victims with fake offers, usually through links or downloads that seem too good to be true. Once the victim clicks or downloads, the attacker can install malware, steal data, or compromise security.

For example, while shopping online, a user may see a pop-up offering the same product at a lower price. Clicking the link may lead to a fake site or download, which then harms the victim’s system or tricks them into spending money. In the end, the promised deal turns out to be fake or replaced with something more expensive.
