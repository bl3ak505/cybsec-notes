# ⚠️ Wireless Threats
The previous sections discussed basic wireless concepts and wireless security mechanisms such as encryption algorithms that secure wireless network communications. To secure wireless networks, a network administrator needs to understand the various possible weaknesses of encryption algorithms, which may lure attackers. The wireless network can be at risk to various types of attacks, including access-control attacks, integrity attacks, confidentiality attacks, availability attacks, and authentication attacks. This section discusses different types of security risks, threats, and attacks associated with wireless networks. 

---
## Access Control Attacks 
Wireless access-control attacks aim to penetrate a network by evading WLAN access-control measures, such as AP MAC filters and Wi-Fi port access controls. There are several types of access-control attacks, including the following. 

### MAC Spoofing
MAC spoofing is an attack where an attacker changes their device’s MAC address to mimic that of an authorized access point or trusted device. By doing this, they can trick hosts on the network into connecting through them. Tools like SMAC make it easy to perform this attack, allowing unauthorized access and potential interception of network traffic.

### AP misconfiguration
AP misconfiguration occurs when critical security settings on an access point are set incorrectly, often unintentionally. Since APs are recognized as legitimate devices, intrusion detection systems usually do not raise alerts, making the issue difficult to spot. A single misconfigured AP can weaken an otherwise secure network, allowing attackers to connect and exploit it without detection. As organizations expand their Wi-Fi networks, such misconfigurations pose a serious risk if security policies are not properly enforced and maintained.

The key elements that play an important role in this kind of attack include the following: 
- **SSID broadcast:** An attacker configures APs to broadcast SSIDs to authorized users. All AP models have their own default SSID, and APs with default configurations using default SSIDs are vulnerable to brute-force dictionary attacks. Even if users enable WEP, an unencrypted SSID broadcasts the password in plaintext.
  
- **Weak password:** Some network administrators incorrectly use SSIDs as basic passwords to verify authorized users. SSIDs act as rudimentary passwords and help network administrators recognize authorized wireless devices in the network.

- **Configuration error:** Configuration errors include errors made during installation, configuration policies on an AP, human errors made while troubleshooting WLAN problems, and security changes not implemented uniformly across an architecture. SSID broadcasting is a configuration error that assists attackers in stealing an SSID, which makes the AP assume that the attacker is attempting a legitimate connection.

### Ad hoc associations
Ad-hoc associations allow Wi-Fi devices to connect directly without using an access point. While convenient for sharing data, this mode is insecure because it lacks strong authentication and encryption. Attackers can force devices into ad-hoc mode, connect easily, and compromise the client. Once inside, they may also use this weak entry point to target and attack the organization’s wired network.

### Promiscuous Client
A promiscuous client attack takes advantage of the way wireless devices automatically connect to the strongest available signal. An attacker sets up a fake access point (AP) with the same SSID as the real network but with a stronger signal, tricking clients into connecting. Once connected, the attacker can intercept and control the victim’s traffic. This method is very similar to an evil twin attack, where a fake AP mimics a legitimate one to capture sensitive data.

### Client mis-association
Client mis-association happens when a wireless device connects to an access point (AP) outside the legitimate network, either by mistake or through an attacker’s rogue AP. This often occurs due to weak Wi-Fi policies, poor coverage, or attractive spoofed SSIDs. Attackers exploit this by setting up a fake AP with the same SSID as the target network to trick users into connecting. Once connected, the attacker can intercept sensitive data like usernames and passwords or launch attacks such as MITM and EAP dictionary attacks, bypassing enterprise security controls.

### Unauthorized association
Unauthorized association is a wireless network threat where devices connect to networks without proper approval. It has two forms: accidental association and malicious association. In malicious association, attackers set up fake access points (soft APs) using laptops or devices to trick victims into connecting, giving them access to steal data, spread malware, or attack the internal network. In accidental association, a device unknowingly connects to a nearby overlapping AP from another organization, which can also expose sensitive information.

## Integrity Attacks 
Integrity Attacks An integrity attack involves changing or altering data during transmission. In wireless integrity attacks, attackers send forged control, management, or data frames over a wireless network to misdirect wireless devices and perform another type of attack such as a DoS attack. The below table summarizes different types of integrity attacks.

| Type of Attack                  | Description                                                                 | Method and Tools                                |
|---------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| Data-Frame Injection            | Constructing and sending forged 802.11 frames.                              | Airpwn-ng, Wperf                                |
| WEP Injection                   | Constructing and sending forged WEP encryption keys.                        | WEP cracking + injection tools                  |
| Bit-Flipping Attacks            | Capturing the frame and flipping random bits in the data payload, modifying the ICV, and sending it to the user. |            |
| Extensible AP Replay            | Capturing 802.1X Extensible Authentication Protocols (e.g., EAP Identity, Success, and Failure) for later replay. | Wireless capture + injection tools between client and AP |
| Data Replay                     | Capturing 802.11 data frames for later (modified) replay.                   | Capture + injection tools                       |
| Initialization Vector Replay Attacks | Deriving the keystream by sending a plaintext message.                   |                                                |
| RADIUS Replay                   | Capturing RADIUS Access-Accept or Reject messages for later replay.         | Ethernet capture + injection tools between AP and authentication server |
| Wireless Network Viruses        | Viruses have a great impact on wireless networks. They can provide an attacker with a simple method to compromise APs. |                              |

## Confidentiality Attacks 
These attacks attempt to intercept confidential information sent over a wireless network, regardless of whether the system transmits data in cleartext or an encrypted format. If the system transmits data in an encrypted format (such as WEP or WPA), an attacker may attempt to break the encryption. The below table summarizes different types of confidentiality attacks on wireless networks.

| Type of Attack     | Description                                                                 | Method and Tools                                     |
|--------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|
| Eavesdropping      | Capturing and decoding unprotected application traffic to obtain potentially sensitive information. | Wireshark, Ettercap, Kismet, commercial analyzers   |
| Traffic Analysis   | Inferring information from the observation of external traffic characteristics. | Wireshark, Ettercap, Snort                          |
| Cracking WEP Key   | Capturing data to recover a WEP key using brute force or Fluhrer-Mantin-Shamir (FMS) cryptanalysis. | Aircrack-ng, WEPCRack                               |
| Evil Twin AP       | Posing as an authorized AP by beaconing the WLAN's SSID to lure users.      | Hostapd, EvilTwinFramework, Wifiphisher             |
| Honeypot AP        | Setting an AP’s SSID to be the same as that of a legitimate AP.             | Manipulating SSID                                   |
| Session Hijacking  | Manipulating the network such that the attacker's host appears to be the desired destination. | Manipulating                                       |
| Masquerading       | Pretending to be an authorized user to gain access to a system.             | Stealing login IDs and passwords, bypassing authentication mechanisms |
| MITM Attack        | Running conventional MITM attack tools on an evil-twin AP to intercept TCP sessions or Secure Sockets Layer (SSL)/Secure Shell (SSH) tunnels. | dsniff, Ettercap, aLTER attack |

## Availability Attacks 
Availability attacks aim at obstructing the delivery of wireless services to legitimate users, either by crippling WLAN resources or by denying them access to these resources. This attack makes wireless network services unavailable to legitimate users. Attackers can perform availability attacks in various ways, obstructing the availability of wireless networks. The below table summarizes different types of availability attacks on wireless networks.

| **Type of Attack** | **Description** | **Method and Tools** |
|--------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Access Point Theft | Physically removing an AP from its installed location. | Shell and/or speed  |
| Disassociation Attacks  | Destroying the connectivity between an AP and client to make the target unavailable to other wireless clients. | Destruction of connectivity  |
| EAP-Failure | Observing a valid 802.1X EAP exchange and then sending EAP-Failure frame. | Airtool Pi |
| Beacon Flood  | Generating thousands of counterfeit 802.11 beacons to make it difficult for clients to find a legitimate AP. | -  |
| Denial-of-Service | Exploiting CSMA/CA CCA mechanism to make a channel appear busy. | Adapter supporting CW Tx mode + low-level utility for continuous transmissions |
| De-authenticate Flood | Flooding client's list of remembered authenticates/disassociates to disconnect users from an AP. | RIP protocol, exploiting AODV and DSR via wormhole and sinkhole attacks |
| Routing Attacks | Distributing routing information to target specific nodes. | -    |
| Authentication Attacks | Sending forged authentication frames from random MACs to target AP's association table. | Airlack  |
| ARP Cache Poisoning Attacks | Creating many attack vectors.  |
| Power Saving Attacks  | Transmitting spoofed TIM/DTIM to clients in power-saving mode, causing DoS. |

## Authentication Attacks
The objective of authentication attacks is to steal the identity of Wi-Fi clients, their personal information, login credentials, etc. to gain unauthorized access to network resources. The below table summarizes different types of authentication attacks on wireless networks.

| **Type of Attack** | **Description** | **Method and Tools**   |
|--------------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| PSK Cracking | Recovering a WPA PSK from captured key handshake frames using a dictionary attack tool. | Aircrack-ng, Fern WiFi Cracker |
| EAP Cracking | Recovering user credentials from captured EAP packets by cracking the NT password hash. | Asleap, THC-LEAPCracker |
| VPN Login Cracking | Gaining VPN credentials (e.g., PPTP password or IPsec PSK) via brute-force attacks. | ike-scan, IKECrack (IPsec), THC-pptp-bruter (PPTP)  |
| Domain Login Cracking  | Cracking NetBIOS password hashes to recover Windows login credentials. | John the Ripper, L0phtCrack, THC-Hydra |

## Honeypot AP Attack 
A honeypot AP attack happens when an attacker sets up a rogue access point using the same SSID as a legitimate network but with a stronger signal. Wireless clients, which automatically search for the best available signal, may unknowingly connect to this fake AP. Once connected, sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, or identity details can be captured by the attacker, creating a serious security risk.

## Wormhole Attack
A wormhole attack is a serious threat in wireless sensor networks that exploit routing protocols like AODV and DSR. In these protocols, a source node sends a Route Request (RREQ) to discover a path to the destination, and the destination responds with a Route Reply (RREP). The source then stores this route and begins sending its data.

In a wormhole attack, an attacker introduces a malicious node that sits within the range of both the source and destination. This node captures the RREQ from the source and quickly tunnels it directly to the destination, bypassing the normal network path. It then does the same for the RREP, sending it back to the source before the legitimate reply arrives. This tricks both source and destination into believing that the malicious node provides the shortest and most direct route.

Once the tunnel is established, the attacker can control all traffic between the two nodes. This allows them to drop, alter, or delay packets, perform selective forwarding, or even launch further attacks such as data manipulation or denial of service. Because the attack creates a fake shortcut in the network, it undermines the normal routing process, affecting the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of communication.

Wormhole attacks are particularly dangerous because they can be carried out without the attacker having to compromise individual nodes. By simply manipulating the routing protocol and creating a deceptive tunnel, the attacker gains significant control over the network traffic, making detection and prevention challenging in real-time wireless environments.

## Sinkhole Attack
A sinkhole attack is a routing-based attack where a malicious or compromised node advertises itself as the best path to the base station in order to attract network traffic. By positioning the malicious node close to the base station, the attacker can manipulate routing information, making neighboring nodes believe that forwarding data through this node is the most efficient route. Once the traffic is attracted, the attacker can sniff, drop, or alter the data packets, effectively performing a data forging attack.

This attack is particularly dangerous because it allows the adversary to control a significant portion of the network’s communication. By intercepting traffic at such a critical point, attackers can compromise the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of data. A sinkhole attack becomes even more powerful when combined with a wormhole attack, where tunneling techniques are used to forward traffic faster than legitimate nodes, making the malicious path appear even more attractive.

Detecting sinkhole attacks is difficult because they blend into normal routing behavior and exploit trust among nodes. The impact is not limited to the routing layer alone but can cascade into higher layers of the OSI model, disrupting applications and services that depend on reliable data delivery. This makes sinkhole attacks a serious threat in wireless sensor networks and other distributed systems.

## Inter-Chip Privilege Escalation/Wireless Co-Existence Attack 
An inter-chip privilege escalation, also known as a wireless co-existence attack, takes advantage of vulnerabilities in the design of wireless chips that handle technologies like Wi-Fi and Bluetooth. Many devices use either separate chips for each technology or a combo chip that supports both. When these chips share resources, attackers can exploit flaws in one chip to compromise the other.

For example, if a Bluetooth chip and a Wi-Fi chip are integrated into a single combo module, an attacker could target the Bluetooth interface, which may have weaker protections, and then use it as a pathway to access the Wi-Fi chip. Once inside, the attacker could capture sensitive information such as credentials, manipulate traffic, or inject malicious data into the communication stream.

This type of attack allows movement across chip boundaries, leading to privilege escalation and broader system compromise. Because these chips often handle critical wireless communication at the hardware level, such attacks can bypass higher-level security measures, making them dangerous and difficult to detect.
