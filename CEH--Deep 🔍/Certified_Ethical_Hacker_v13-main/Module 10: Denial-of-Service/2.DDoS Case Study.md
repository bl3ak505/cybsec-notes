# 📚 DDoS Case Study
DDoS attacks are sophisticated and complex attacks based on DoS and multiple distributed attack sources. In a DDoS attack, a large number of compromised computers (zombies) interrupt or suspend network services. This section presents a case study of a DDoS attack.

---
## DDoS Attack
In a DDoS attack, attackers use a group of compromised systems (bots or zombies) usually infected with Trojans to perform a DoS attack on a target system or network resource.

![image](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/8bcd5295-892c-44d6-a35b-26d1cee61d2e)

In this attack, a hacker hosts the **HOIC (High Orbit Ion Cannon)** DDoS tool on their own or a hacked web server and shares it through social media or search engines using a malicious link. People who download it, known as **“volunteers,”** connect to the hacker through an **IRC channel** and wait for instructions.

The hacker then commands them to flood a target website (like PayPal or MasterCard) with traffic. When many volunteers send requests at once, the target server gets overwhelmed and crashes, making it unavailable even for real users. This is how a **DDoS attack** is launched using HOIC and social engineering.

## Hackers Advertise Links for Downloading Botnets 
Hackers advertise botnets on various blogs, search engines, social networking sites, emails, and so on with download links. Hackers also use fake updates and security alerts to trick the victim into downloading the malware. The intention in doing so is to spread the botnet and increase the size of the attack network. ## Use of Mobile Devices as Botnets for Launching DDoS Attacks 
**Mobile Devices as Botnets for DDoS Attacks**
Android devices are often targeted by attackers due to weak security and risky apps found in third-party stores. Hackers hide malware like Trojans and RATs inside modified APK files and upload them to platforms like the Google Play Store. These fake apps look harmless but contain hidden malicious servers.

Once a user installs the infected app, the device becomes part of the attacker’s **mobile botnet**. The attacker can then use it to launch **DDoS attacks**, steal data, or inject harmful content into websites. This method allows attackers to silently control many mobile devices for large-scale cyberattacks.

## DDoS Case Study: HTTP/2 ‘Rapid Reset’ Attack on Google Cloud 
🔗Source: [https://cloud.google.com]

In **September 2023**, Google’s DDoS response team stopped a massive **398 million requests per second** (rps) DDoS attack—**7.5 times bigger** than any past attack. It used a new method called **HTTP/2 "Rapid Reset"**, which targets websites and internet services by overwhelming them with fast, repeated requests to crash systems. This case highlights how DDoS attacks are growing in power and evolving in technique, posing serious threats to online infrastructure.

### ▪️ Attack Timeline
The DDoS attack began in late August 2023 and peaked for a few minutes before the end of September. It targeted Google services and clients by flooding servers with TCP reset packets to disrupt connections.

### ▪️ Attack Mechanism
Attackers exploited a flaw in the **HTTP/2 protocol** using a method called **Rapid Reset**. By rapidly creating and resetting many HTTP/2 streams over a single TCP connection, they overloaded servers.

### ▪️ Google’s Response
Google’s strong edge infrastructure quickly blocked the attack. They upgraded their systems and shared real-time threat data with other companies. The industry worked together to fix the issue, and a high-severity CVE (**CVE-2023-44487**, CVSS 7.5) was assigned to the vulnerability.
