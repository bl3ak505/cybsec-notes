## Windows potato family 

The “potato” family is a set of Windows local privilege escalation techniques that typically turn a service account or low-privileged context into **NT AUTHORITY\SYSTEM** by abusing impersonation and NTLM/RPC/DCOM-related behavior. The family started with Hot Potato and later expanded into variants such as RottenPotato, JuicyPotato, RoguePotato, PrintSpoofer, SweetPotato, GodPotato, and newer local NTLM-based techniques.

## Core idea

Most potato exploits depend on having a token or service context with impersonation-capable privileges, especially SeImpersonatePrivilege. The attacker triggers or relays an authentication flow, captures or redirects it, and then impersonates a higher-privileged security context to obtain SYSTEM-level code execution.

## Main variants

- **Hot Potato**: Early technique using NTLM relay and local spoofing to escalate from service accounts.
    
- **RottenPotato / RottenPotatoNG**: Built around token impersonation and service-account-to-SYSTEM escalation.
    
- **JuicyPotato**: A popular COM/DCOM-based escalation method for service accounts with impersonation rights.
    
- **RoguePotato**: Uses a fake OXID resolver / RPC trick to reach SYSTEM under suitable conditions.
    
- **PrintSpoofer / SweetPotato / Generic Potato**: Variants that abuse different local coercion or impersonation paths, often when SeImpersonatePrivilege is present.[](https://jlajara.gitlab.io/Potatoes_Windows_Privesc)
    
- **LocalPotato**: A newer local NTLM authentication weakness involving context swapping in LSASS.
    

## When they work

These techniques are most useful when the compromised account is a Windows service account or otherwise has SeImpersonatePrivilege. They are less about “finding a bug in the app” and more about exploiting Windows authentication and impersonation plumbing to elevate privileges.

## What defenders check

A practical defender-focused checklist is:

- Review which service accounts have SeImpersonatePrivilege.
    
- Limit unnecessary service permissions.
    
- Reduce exposure of coercion surfaces such as print spooler, RPC, or COM/DCOM paths where feasible.
    
- Patch and harden against known potato variants as Microsoft and researchers publish fixes and mitigations.
    

## Compact study note

A simple way to remember the family is: **“coerce, relay, impersonate, SYSTEM.”** Most variants differ mainly in _how_ they coerce or relay authentication, not in the end goal.
